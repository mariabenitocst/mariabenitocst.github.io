Research Interests

Due to our position in the Galactic plane of the Milky Way (MW), at 8 kpc from the Galactic center (GC), we have access to full phase-space information for a large number of individual stars within the Galaxy. This detailed view of the Galaxy allows studies of the different components (i.e. stellar bulge, stellar disc, gas and stellar halo) that constitute our Galaxy and how these components have come to be.

My research has been focused on determining the distribution of dark matter (DM) in our Galaxy using kinematic tracers of the total gravitational potential. The detailed knowledge of the DM distribution in our Galaxy is important for understanding the interplay between baryons and DM in the processes involved in galaxy formation and evolution. It is further a key element for experiments that aim to directly or indirectly detect the DM particle due to theoretically predicted non-negligible, weak interactions between DM and Standard Model particles.

As a member of the Cherenkov Telescope Array (CTA) collaboration, I have been working in the characterization of the Large Magellanic Cloud, a satellite galaxy of the MW, at TeV energies.

In the following, I describe some of the topics I am & have been recently involved in.

Mass Distribution in the Galactic Bulge Region

The DM is a subleading component in the central region of our Galaxy (i.e. inner 3 kpc) which is dynamically dominated by baryons. The actual content and density profile of the DM in such central region is at the center of a long-standing debate on whether the DM profile of galaxies is "cored" or "cusped". Our ignorance about the morphology of the bulge and the normalisation of the visible component prevents strong conclusions on the DM distribution in the inner 3 kpc.

Credit: APS/R. Lang

Particle Dark Matter Constraints: the Effect of Galactic Uncertainties

The interpretation of results from indirect and direct DM searches depends on the adopted DM density profile in the astrophysical target. Particularly, the reconstruction of the DM profile in the MW proceeds from astrophysical observations, all affected by quantifiable uncertainties. I have worked on a full data-driven analysis that quantifies actual astrophysical uncertainties on the determination of the DM profile in our Galaxy. The likelihood profile of this analysis, available here, can be easily used to propagate astrophysical uncertainties into the DM particle parameter space.

Credit: GAIA/ESO

Testing Modified Gravity Theories with the Milky Way

Alternative proposals to explain the mismatch between the observed dynamical mass and that inferred by observations of the visible component rely on modifications of the theory of gravity. The MW offers an unique opportunity for testing such modifications on the scale of spiral galaxies. I have tested John Moffat's Modified Gravity theory (MOG) by comparing the observed gravitational potential as a function of the galactocentric distance with the potential generated by the baryonic component under different flavours of the MOG theory. We concluded that in none of its present formulation, the MOG theory is able to explain the observed RC of the MW. Here are some slides of this work.

Credit: Bucksnort Observatory

Large Magellanic Cloud Studies with CTA

The Large Magellanic Cloud (LMC) is an active star forming galaxy. This, together with its proximity and the fact that it is seen almost faced-on, turns the LMC a valuable target for gamma-ray studies. We have studied the forecasts on detecting already known gamma-ray sources (i.e. point sources, individual extended sources and the diffuse emission produced by the propagation of cosmic rays) in the LMC with the CTA's Southern Array. We have further characterized the gamma-ray signal expected from the LMC due to DM annihilation and the corresponding upper limits on the thermally-average DM self-annihilation cross-section as a function of the DM mass.
