Research Interests

Our Galaxy, the Milky Way (MW), plays an important role for understanding galaxy formation & evolution. It is also a key element for experiments that aim to unravel the very nature of the dark matter particle. Due to our position within the Galactic plane, at 8 kpc from the Galactic center, we have access to full phase-space information for a large number of individual stars. This allows detailed studies of the different components (i.e. stellar bulge, stellar disc, gas and stellar halo) that constitute our Galaxy and how they have come to be.

My research has been focused on determining the distribution of dark matter (DM) in our Galaxy by means of the rotation curve mass modelling. Objects in circular orbits aroung the Galactic center trace the total gravitational potential. By subtracting to the observed velocities the expected velocities by the pure effect of gravity of the visible component (stars and gas), we can infer constraints on the distribution of dark matter.
A precise determination of the DM profile in our Galaxy is important for placing the MW in a cosmological context. Confronting the observed DM distribution with results from zoom-in N-body simulations might further help to understand the dynamical interplay between baryons and DM. Furthermore, the distribution of DM in the MW is a key element for experiments that aim to unravel the very nature of the DM particle.

As a member of the Cherenkov Telescope Array (CTA) collaboration, I have been working in the characterization of the Large Magellanic Cloud, a satellite galaxy of the Milky Way, at TeV energies.

Mass Distribution in the Galactic Bulge Region

Dark matter is a subleading component in the central region of our Galaxy (i.e. inner 3 kpc) which is dynamically dominated by baryons. The actual content and density profile of the DM in such region is at the center of a long-standing debate on whether the DM profile of galaxies is "cored" or "cusped". Sizeable uncertainties on the actual shape and normalization of the stellar morphology prevent drawing general conclusions on the inner slope of the DM density profile, and both core and cusp DM density profiles are allowed.

Credit: APS/R. Lang

Particle Dark Matter Constraints: the Effect of Galactic Uncertainties

The interpretation of results from indirect and direct DM searches depends on the adopted DM density profile in the astrophysical target. Particularly, the reconstruction of the DM profile in the MW proceeds from astrophysical observations, all affected by quantifiable uncertainties. I have worked on a full data-driven analysis that quantifies actual astrophysical uncertainties on the determination of the DM profile in our Galaxy. The likelihood profile of this analysis is a practical tool to propagate uncertainties on astrophysical quantities into the DM particle parameter space.

Credit: GAIA/ESO

Testing Modified Gravity Theories with the Milky Way

Alternative proposals to explain the mismatch between the observed dynamical mass and that inferred by observations of the visible component rely on modifications of the theory of gravity. The MW offers an unique opportunity for testing such modifications on the scale of spiral galaxies by means of the observed rotation curve. By comparing the gravitational potential as a function of galactocentric distance with the potential that is expected to be generated by the visible component only, we can test the validity of the underlying gravity theory.

Credit: Bucksnort Observatory

Large Magellanic Cloud Studies with CTA

The Large Magellanic Cloud (LMC) is an active star forming galaxy. This, together with its proximity and the fact that it is seen almost faced-on, turns the LMC a valuable target for gamma-ray studies. We have studied the forecasts on detecting already known gamma-ray sources (i.e. point sources, individual extended sources and the diffuse emission produced by the propation of cosmic rays within the source) in the LMC with the CTA's Southern Array. We have further characterized the gamma-ray signal expected from the LMC due to DM annihilation and the corresponding upper limits on the thermally-average DM self-annihilation cross-section as a function of the DM mass.
